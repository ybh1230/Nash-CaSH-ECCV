{\LARGE \bf \method: Equilibrium-Guided Semantic Coordination for\\
Training-Free High-Resolution Video Generation}

\vspace{0.6em}
{\large Anonymous ECCV Submission}

\vspace{0.8em}

\begin{abstract}
Training-free high-resolution video generation requires a pretrained video diffusion model to operate far beyond the spatial token distribution observed during pretraining. The central challenge is not only computational. A high-resolution denoising step must preserve prompt-level structure while recovering native-resolution visual evidence such as facial details, object boundaries, and fine textures. Existing inference-time rules usually resolve this conflict with fixed attention choices or fixed fusion coefficients, which cannot adapt to prompt content, denoising stage, token location, or cache reliability. This paper introduces \method, an equilibrium-guided semantic coordination principle for high-resolution video diffusion. At each cross-attention layer, \method observes two forms of semantic evidence: contextual evidence for scene-level structure and native-grid evidence for high-frequency detail. Their reliability is measured from nonnegative query-response alignment and token-relative confidence. The final semantic authority is obtained by solving a disagreement-aware Nash bargaining problem over a shared authority budget. The solution is not merely raw softmax reweighting: it is scale-invariant, proportionally fair over surplus authority, bounded by explicit disagreement points, and has controlled sensitivity to log-reliability noise. The method requires no training, no additional data, and no model-weight modification.
\end{abstract}

]

\section{Introduction}

Text-to-video diffusion models have made rapid progress in synthesizing coherent short videos from natural language prompts. However, their learned generation prior is usually tied to a moderate native resolution. When the same denoiser is directly applied to 1080P synthesis, the number of spatial tokens increases dramatically. This shift often causes blurred details, unstable small objects, distorted faces, repeated structures, and weak temporal consistency. The problem is especially visible in close-up shots, dense backgrounds, and videos that require both large-scale layout and local texture fidelity.

A natural strategy is to use inference-time refinement without retraining the model. High-resolution inference can reuse a pretrained generator and avoid costly high-resolution video datasets. Yet the inference rule must decide how semantic information is injected into the high-resolution latent. Contextual attention preserves prompt-level meaning, identity, object count, and scene layout. Native-grid evidence preserves local visual fidelity under a spatial regime closer to the model's training distribution. These two forms of evidence are complementary, but they are not equally reliable at every token or denoising step. A fixed rule may over-trust contextual semantics and wash out details, or over-trust native-grid evidence and fragment the scene.

This paper argues that high-resolution refinement should be formulated as a semantic coordination problem. At each cross-attention layer, the denoiser must assign one unit of semantic authority to multiple forms of evidence. This authority determines how strongly each evidence form influences the final hidden representation. Instead of setting this authority by hand, we seek a principle that is adaptive, training-free, and stable during diffusion sampling.

We propose \method, an equilibrium-guided semantic coordination method. The key idea is to treat contextual semantic evidence and native-grid visual evidence as two cooperative terms sharing a constrained semantic resource. Their current reliability is measured from attention behavior: nonnegative alignment indicates whether an evidence response is compatible with the requesting token, while token-relative confidence indicates which evidence form is more active at the same location. Given these reliability values, \method solves a disagreement-aware Nash bargaining objective and obtains a closed-form authority allocation. The solution increases the authority of more reliable evidence while preserving minimum authority for both contextual structure and native-grid detail.

This formulation is different from a heuristic weighted sum. The mixture coefficient is not a manually selected constant. It is the equilibrium solution of a defined semantic coordination objective with explicit disagreement points. The method therefore provides a clear rationale for when contextual structure should dominate and when native-grid detail should be trusted. It is also compatible with cached inference: expensive contextual evidence can be reused across denoising steps, while the equilibrium rule still evaluates whether the cached response deserves authority at the current step.

\method is designed for practical deployment. It does not finetune the video model, add a learned controller, or require extra annotations. The added computation consists of lightweight reliability observation and a closed-form allocation inside attention layers. This makes the approach suitable for 1080P generation where both quality and runtime matter.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We formulate training-free high-resolution video refinement as equilibrium-guided semantic coordination rather than fixed attention selection.
    \item We introduce attention-grounded reliability measures using nonnegative query-response alignment and token-relative confidence, requiring no learned scoring network.
    \item We derive a closed-form disagreement-aware Nash authority allocation and show scale invariance, proportional fairness, bounded extreme behavior, and log-noise sensitivity.
    \item We introduce authority momentum to reduce step-to-step authority jitter during cached video refinement.
    \item We provide an experimental protocol, ablations, and visualization design for evaluating 1080P quality, temporal consistency, and runtime under matched seeds.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Video Diffusion Models}

Diffusion models have become a dominant approach for visual generation \cite{ho2020ddpm,rombach2022ldm}. Transformer-based denoisers further improve scalability by using attention to model long-range dependencies \cite{vaswani2017attention,peebles2023dit}. In video generation, attention is crucial because appearance, motion, and prompt semantics must remain coordinated across both space and time. However, attention cost grows quickly with token count, and high-resolution video inference introduces a severe token-distribution shift.

\subsection{Training-Free High-Resolution Inference}

Training-free high-resolution inference aims to improve output resolution without collecting high-resolution data or finetuning large generative models. Existing strategies include latent upsampling, noise-based refinement, local attention, token reuse, and cached computation \cite{meng2021sdedit,bolya2023tome,ma2024deepcache}. These methods are attractive because they can be applied to pretrained models. Their limitation is that all decisions must be made during inference, making the rule that coordinates contextual semantics and native-grid evidence especially important.

\subsection{Locality and Semantic Consistency}

Local computation is useful because small neighborhoods are closer to the model's native training regime and better preserve high-frequency detail \cite{liu2021swin,hassani2023nat}. Nevertheless, video generation cannot be solved by local evidence alone. A prompt specifies identity, count, layout, background, attributes, and motion. These constraints require contextual semantic coordination. The central challenge is therefore not whether to use contextual or native-grid evidence, but how to allocate authority between them adaptively.

\subsection{Caching in Diffusion Sampling}

Caching accelerates diffusion sampling by reusing intermediate computations across steps \cite{ma2024deepcache}. In high-resolution video generation, contextual evidence can be expensive, so reusing it is valuable. However, cached responses may become stale as the latent evolves. \method treats cached contextual evidence as an evidence term whose authority must still be justified by current reliability. Thus, caching improves efficiency while equilibrium coordination controls whether the cached response remains trustworthy.

\subsection{Nash Equilibrium and Bargaining}

Nash bargaining provides an axiomatic solution for allocating a shared resource among cooperative terms with positive utilities \cite{nash1950bargaining,nash1951noncooperative}. Maximizing the Nash product balances efficiency and non-collapse: a term with higher utility receives more resource, but the solution discourages assigning all authority to one term when multiple terms remain useful. This property matches high-resolution semantic coordination, where both contextual structure and native-grid detail are needed for stable 1080P video synthesis.

\section{Method}

\subsection{High-Resolution Refinement}

Given a text prompt $c$, a pretrained video diffusion model generates or refines a latent video. Let $z_t$ denote the latent at denoising step $t$. In a cross-attention layer, visual queries interact with textual keys and values to inject prompt semantics. At high resolution, a visual token requires both contextual semantic structure and native-grid visual detail.

For token $i$, let
\begin{equation}
    O_c(i), O_n(i) \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times D}
\end{equation}
be two candidate cross-attention responses before output projection. The subscript $c$ denotes contextual semantic evidence, and $n$ denotes native-grid evidence. $H$ is the number of attention heads and $D$ is the head dimension. The final response is
\begin{equation}
    O(i)=a(i)O_c(i)+(1-a(i))O_n(i),
    \label{eq:mix}
\end{equation}
where $a(i)$ is the semantic authority assigned to contextual evidence. The goal is to determine $a(i)$ adaptively and without training.

\subsection{Semantic Evidence}

\noindent\textbf{Contextual semantic evidence.}
This evidence preserves prompt-level structure, object identity, object count, long-range layout, and broad temporal consistency. It can be obtained from full-context cross-attention or from cached full-context attention responses. It is especially important during early denoising and in regions where global layout or identity constraints dominate.

\noindent\textbf{Native-grid visual evidence.}
This evidence preserves local texture, boundaries, and high-frequency details under a spatial regime closer to the model's native training distribution. It can be obtained from native-scale windowed attention or another local evidence construction. It is especially important during later denoising, close-up synthesis, and regions with fine structures.

Both evidence forms are produced by the same pretrained model at inference time. The proposed method does not create separate networks. It only decides how much authority each evidence form should receive.

\subsection{Attention-Grounded Reliability}

The equilibrium rule requires positive reliability values, but these values should not be learned from additional data. \method computes them directly from the current attention response. For evidence type $x\in\{c,n\}$, let $Q_x(i)$ be the query vector and $O_x(i)$ be the candidate response for token $i$ and one attention head.

First, query-response alignment measures whether the response is semantically compatible with the token that requested it:
\begin{equation}
    A_x(i)=\max\left(0,\cos(O_x(i),Q_x(i))\right)+\epsilon .
    \label{eq:alignment}
\end{equation}
Unlike a shifted cosine score, negative directions are not treated as medium reliability. A response that points against the query direction is regarded as conflicting evidence and receives near-zero alignment.

Second, confidence is measured relatively between the two evidence forms at the same token:
\begin{equation}
    E_x(i)=\operatorname{RMS}(O_x(i)),\qquad
    C_x(i)=
    \frac{E_x(i)}
    {E_c(i)+E_n(i)+\epsilon}.
    \label{eq:confidence}
\end{equation}
This token-relative confidence avoids the spatial non-stationarity problem of a global RMS mean. A foreground face token and a flat background token are not forced to share the same global activation scale.

The final reliability is
\begin{equation}
    r_x(i)=\pi_x\left(A_x(i)+C_x(i)+\epsilon\right),
    \label{eq:reliability}
\end{equation}
where $\pi_x$ is a weak evidence prior. By default, $\pi_c=1.25$ and $\pi_n=1.0$. This prior protects prompt-level structure, while native-grid evidence can still receive most of the surplus authority when its observed reliability is stronger.

\subsection{Disagreement-Aware Nash Coordination}

For each token, contextual and native-grid evidence share one unit of semantic authority. A direct unconstrained allocation can collapse to one evidence form, which is undesirable in high-resolution video refinement: prompt structure and native-grid detail are both necessary. We therefore define a disagreement point $(d_c,d_n)$ with $d_c\geq0$, $d_n\geq0$, and $d_c+d_n<1$. This point represents the minimum authority preserved when bargaining fails. The remaining surplus authority is
\begin{equation}
    S=1-d_c-d_n.
\end{equation}
In our implementation, $d_c=a_{\min}$ and $d_n=1-a_{\max}$, so $S=a_{\max}-a_{\min}$.

The Nash coordination objective is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \max_{a_c,a_n}\quad&
    r_c(i)\log(a_c-d_c+\epsilon)
    +r_n(i)\log(a_n-d_n+\epsilon)\\
    \text{s.t.}\quad&
    a_c+a_n=1,\quad a_c>d_c,\quad a_n>d_n .
\end{aligned}
    \label{eq:nash_disagreement}
\end{equation}
The final response uses $a(i)=a_c(i)$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:mix}.

\begin{proposition}[Equilibrium authority and proportional fairness]
Ignoring $\epsilon$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:nash_disagreement} has the unique solution
\begin{equation}
    a_c^*(i)=d_c+S
    \frac{r_c(i)}
    {r_c(i)+r_n(i)},\qquad
    a_n^*(i)=d_n+S
    \frac{r_n(i)}
    {r_c(i)+r_n(i)} .
    \label{eq:closed_disagreement}
\end{equation}
Moreover, the solution is Pareto efficient, symmetric over the surplus when $r_c=r_n$ and $d_c=d_n$, invariant to common positive rescaling of reliabilities, and proportionally fair:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{x\in\{c,n\}}
    r_x(i)
    \frac{a_x-a_x^*}
    {a_x^*-d_x}
    \leq 0
    \quad
    \text{for any feasible } (a_c,a_n).
    \label{eq:prop_fair}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textbf{Proof sketch.}
Let $s_x=a_x-d_x$ be surplus authority. The feasible set becomes $s_c+s_n=S$ and $s_x>0$. The objective $\sum_x r_x\log s_x$ is strictly concave, so the KKT conditions are sufficient and necessary. The stationarity condition gives $r_x/s_x=\lambda$, hence $s_x=S r_x/(r_c+r_n)$, yielding Eq.~\eqref{eq:closed_disagreement}. Pareto efficiency follows from using the full authority budget. Symmetry and scale invariance follow directly from Eq.~\eqref{eq:closed_disagreement}. Proportional fairness follows from concavity: for any feasible $s$,
\begin{equation}
    \nabla\left(\sum_x r_x\log s_x\right)_{s=s^*}^{\top}(s-s^*)\leq 0,
\end{equation}
which is Eq.~\eqref{eq:prop_fair}. \hfill $\square$

\subsection{Why This Is Not Raw Softmax}

For the special case $d_c=d_n=0$ and $\tau=1$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:closed_disagreement} has the same numerical form as a softmax applied to $\log r_x$. This is an implementation convenience, not the theoretical claim. It differs from directly applying $\operatorname{softmax}(r_c,r_n)$ in three ways.

First, the Nash rule is scale-invariant: multiplying all reliabilities by a positive constant does not change the allocation. Raw softmax lacks this property because $\operatorname{softmax}(\gamma r_c,\gamma r_n)$ becomes sharper as $\gamma$ grows even when the relative evidence has not changed. Second, disagreement points allocate only the surplus authority, preventing collapse to zero contextual or zero native-grid evidence. In the extreme case $r_n/r_c\to\infty$, the contextual authority approaches $d_c$ rather than zero; when $r_c/r_n\to\infty$, it approaches $1-d_n$ rather than one. Third, Eq.~\eqref{eq:prop_fair} gives a proportional-fairness condition: no alternative feasible allocation can improve the reliability-weighted relative authority of one evidence form without a compensating loss to the other. These properties are absent from a heuristic softmax gate unless they are added manually.

Temperature is applied to reliabilities as $\tilde r_x=r_x^{1/\tau}$. With $\theta=\log r_c-\log r_n$, the two-evidence allocation satisfies
\begin{equation}
    a_c^*=d_c+S\sigma(\theta/\tau),\qquad
    \left|
    \frac{\partial a_c^*}{\partial \theta}
    \right|
    \leq
    \frac{S}{4\tau}.
    \label{eq:stability}
\end{equation}
Thus, the authority map has a bounded sensitivity to multiplicative reliability noise in log space. Smaller surplus $S$ and larger temperature $\tau$ both improve stability, while larger $S$ allows more aggressive adaptation.

\subsection{Temporal Authority Stabilization}

Video refinement should not allow authority maps to fluctuate abruptly across denoising steps. In cached inference, \method therefore applies an exponential moving average to the authority map:
\begin{equation}
    \bar a_t(i)=
    \beta\bar a_{t-1}(i)+(1-\beta)a_t^*(i),
    \label{eq:momentum}
\end{equation}
where $\beta\in[0,1)$ is the authority momentum. This step is applied per attention layer, head, and token. It reduces step-to-step authority jitter while preserving the fixed point when the reliability estimates are stable.

\subsection{Multi-Evidence Extension}

Although this paper uses two evidence forms, the formulation naturally extends to $K$ evidence types, such as temporal evidence or style evidence. Let $d_k$ be the disagreement authority and $r_k$ be the reliability of evidence type $k$, with $S=1-\sum_k d_k>0$. The Nash objective
\begin{equation}
    \max_{\{a_k\}}
    \sum_{k=1}^{K} r_k\log(a_k-d_k)
    \quad
    \text{s.t.}\quad
    \sum_{k=1}^{K}a_k=1,\quad a_k>d_k
\end{equation}
has the closed-form solution
\begin{equation}
    a_k^*=d_k+S\frac{r_k}{\sum_j r_j}.
    \label{eq:multi}
\end{equation}
This extension is important conceptually: \method is not tied to a hand-crafted two-way gate. It is a general semantic authority allocation rule.

\subsection{Cache-Compatible Inference}

Contextual evidence can be expensive at high resolution. \method supports cached inference by refreshing contextual evidence every $P$ steps and reusing it between refreshes. Let $t'$ be the most recent refresh step. At step $t$, the response is
\begin{equation}
    O_t(i)=
    a_t(i)O_{c,t'}(i)
    +(1-a_t(i))O_{n,t}(i).
    \label{eq:cached}
\end{equation}
Native-grid evidence remains current, while contextual evidence can be cached. Crucially, cached evidence does not receive fixed authority. Its authority is still determined by Eq.~\eqref{eq:reliability}--Eq.~\eqref{eq:bounded}, so stale contextual responses can be weakened when current native-grid evidence becomes more reliable.

\subsection{Algorithm}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Generate or initialize a video latent from prompt $c$.
    \item Upsample or refine the latent to the target 1080P grid.
    \item At each cross-attention layer, compute contextual response $O_c$ and native-grid response $O_n$.
    \item Compute reliability values $r_c$ and $r_n$ from alignment and confidence.
    \item Solve the closed-form disagreement-aware Nash authority allocation.
    \item Smooth the authority map with momentum during cached refinement.
    \item Fuse the two responses using Eq.~\eqref{eq:mix}.
    \item Continue denoising and decode the final high-resolution video.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

The main goal is to test whether equilibrium-guided semantic coordination improves high-resolution generation compared with fixed inference rules. All methods should use the same pretrained model, prompt set, random seeds, precision, frame number, denoising steps, guidance scale, and target resolution. When possible, the same low-resolution initialization should be reused across methods.

We evaluate at $1920\times1088$, reported as the 1080P setting because the height is rounded to satisfy latent-grid divisibility. Unless otherwise stated, videos contain 81 frames, high-resolution refinement uses 35 denoising steps, and the cache interval is $P=2$.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting the Nash Rule}

At the implementation level, the final cross-attention response is a mixture of two tensors. The contribution is the rule that determines the authority coefficient. \method does not claim that any normalized gate is automatically a Nash solution. Instead, it defines semantic evidence, nonnegative reliabilities, disagreement authority, a feasible surplus set, and a Nash objective. This distinction matters because the resulting rule is invariant to common reliability scaling, allocates only the surplus authority, satisfies proportional fairness, and has bounded sensitivity in log-reliability space. A raw softmax gate can be included as an ablation, but it does not provide these properties unless they are imposed externally.

\subsection{Limitations}

\method depends on the quality of its reliability observation. Nonnegative alignment avoids assigning high reliability to directionally conflicting responses, and token-relative confidence reduces global foreground/background bias. Nevertheless, these two cues may not capture all forms of semantic reliability. Authority momentum reduces step-to-step jitter, but it is not a full temporal correspondence model across moving objects. Future work could add explicit temporal evidence as a third evidence type using Eq.~\eqref{eq:multi}. The disagreement point is also a design choice: it encodes a minimum level of contextual and native-grid authority and should be reported with ablations. When the pretrained model lacks the ability to represent a concept, equilibrium coordination cannot recover missing knowledge.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented \method, an equilibrium-guided semantic coordination method for training-free high-resolution video generation. The method treats contextual structure and native-grid detail as complementary evidence forms sharing one unit of semantic authority. Their reliability is computed directly from attention behavior, and the final authority is obtained through a disagreement-aware Nash bargaining solution. The resulting rule is scale-invariant, proportionally fair over surplus authority, stable under bounded log-reliability perturbations, and compatible with cached 1080P refinement. This provides a principled alternative to fixed inference rules and direct softmax gating.
